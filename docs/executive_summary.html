Executive Summary

RFC to Encompass Data Mapping
Methodology Recommendation

A BSA consultant assessment of the current mapping engagement structure, what is working, what is not, and what needs to change before further mapping work proceeds. The recommendation represents the faster, cleaner, and more defensible path to a complete and auditable migration spec.

Methodology Decision Needed Carrington Acquisition Initiative RFC Origination Integration Encompass System of Record
📋
Situation
▾

Carrington engaged a BSA consultant to map loan data from Reliance First Capital's (RFC) proprietary LOS to Encompass as the go-forward system of record following the acquisition (announced October 31, 2025, expected to close Q1 2026). The acquisition adds a direct-to-consumer origination channel to Carrington's existing retail, wholesale, and correspondent businesses.

RFC is a call-center driven, DTC lender with six origination centers, over 100 licensed Mortgage Analysts, and approximately $1B per year in origination volume. They originate FHA, VA, USDA, conventional, jumbo, Non-QM, and second-lien products. Their technology is a proprietary, in-house LOS with no vendor name, no published specifications, and no written data dictionary.

The goal is to produce a field-level mapping specification that governs how RFC's loan data moves into Encompass, covering every material field in the loan lifecycle from application through servicing.

⚠
Core Finding
▾

After reviewing the current engagement structure, the available artifacts, and the nature of RFC's technology stack, there is a critical finding to surface before further mapping work proceeds.

The Problem

The current approach frames this engagement as a direct database-to-database mapping: match RFC SQL columns to Carrington Encompass fields. That framing is creating unnecessary difficulty and will produce lower-quality results than an alternative approach already available. RFC's LOS has no vendor documentation, no published MISMO specifications, and no validated data dictionary. The schemas provided to date are informal, inconsistently documented, and have not been confirmed against what the system actually stores and exchanges in production. A direct column-to-column mapping under these conditions means we are producing educated guesses dressed as specifications. In a regulated data migration context, that is a meaningful risk.

This is not a failure of the team. It is a structural issue with how the engagement was scoped relative to what artifacts exist on the RFC side. Catching and correcting this now, before significant additional work is invested, is the right call.

✦
A Better Path Already Exists
▾

RFC cannot originate, close, or deliver loans without producing industry-standard MISMO datasets. This is not optional. Every AUS submission, every GSE delivery, every closing, and every URLA generates a MISMO XML export that maps RFC's internal fields to a known, auditable MISMO container. Those exports exist today and they are already doing the translation work we are trying to recreate manually from incomplete spreadsheets.

One mapping row per field. Three columns. Auditable, industry-standard, and defensible.

Why this works: Encompass is MISMO-native. Every Encompass field ID has a corresponding MISMO path, and that mapping is published and maintained. RFC's MISMO exports are the spec that was never written. The UCD, ULDD, DU/LPA casefile, and URLA collectively reveal how RFC's internal data maps to MISMO containers. We extract the crosswalk from the exports rather than guessing at it from informal spreadsheets.

The MISMO exports RFC already produces routinely:

UCD XML
Uniform Closing Dataset — Every closed loan has one. Maps closing-side fields to MISMO containers.
ULDD XML
Uniform Loan Delivery Dataset — GSE delivery dataset. Maps delivery fields for every Fannie/Freddie loan.
DU / LPA Casefile
AUS Casefile XML — MISMO 3.4. Every AUS submission produces one. Maps application and underwriting fields.
URLA / 1003
Uniform Residential Loan Application — MISMO 3.4. Maps the full application dataset.
iLAD / Boarding File
MISMO 3.5 — In-house servicing boarding handoff. Distinct mapping block for RFC's in-house servicing.

What Is Blocking Progress
▾

Two categories of blockers are preventing high-confidence mapping work, regardless of methodology. Items 1 and 2 are hard blockers. Nothing maps cleanly without them.

1
RFC LOS Database Schema / DDL Export
Read-only access to a non-production RFC LOS database with a schema or DDL export. This is the actual data dictionary that was never written down. Every table, column, data type, and relationship lives here.
Owner: RFC Engineering / DBA Team
2
Sample MISMO XML Exports (3-5 Closed Loans)
UCD, ULDD, and DU/LPA casefile XML from 3 to 5 real closed loans, de-identified if needed. These files already exist and are produced routinely. They are the fastest path to an accurate mapping of RFC's internal fields.
Owner: RFC Closing / Underwriting / Secondary
3
RFC API Contract (WSDL or OpenAPI/Swagger)
Identifies the logical object model even without direct database access. If the LOS is SOAP-based, a WSDL is needed. If REST-based, an OpenAPI/Swagger spec. RFC engineering can confirm which applies.
Owner: RFC Engineering
4
1-2 Hours with the RFC LOS Builder or Maintainer
Undocumented does not mean unknown. The person who built or currently maintains the RFC LOS has the tribal knowledge needed to resolve naming conventions, derived fields, and system quirks that no document captures.
Owner: RFC Engineering or Contracted Dev Shop
5
Encompass Field Dictionary for the Carrington Instance
Every Encompass field ID in the mapping must be validated against the actual Carrington target instance, not generic Encompass documentation. This is a destination-side blocker owned internally by Carrington.
Owner: Carrington Encompass Administrator

Three Asks from Leadership
▾

Three approvals will unlock the engagement and set it on the correct track to produce a complete, high-confidence mapping specification.

1
Endorse the MISMO-Pivot Methodology
Approve RFC source field to MISMO XPath to Encompass Field ID as the official mapping architecture for this engagement. This is the correct approach for an undocumented source system against a MISMO-native target. It produces a more auditable, industry-standard, and defensible deliverable than a direct DB-to-DB column match.
2
Assign Artifact Owners and Set a Delivery Deadline
The five items listed in the blockers section each have a named owner. Leadership needs to assign accountability and a delivery date. The DDL schema dump and MISMO XML samples from RFC are on the critical path. Without them, confidence in the mapping output cannot exceed medium for most field rows.
3
Approve the Shared Mapping and Tracking Tool
RFC cannot access Carrington's ADO or JIRA. Carrington cannot access RFC's internal tools. The current email and spreadsheet exchange is producing slow and difficult-to-audit results. A shared, always-available Vercel URL will serve as the neutral collaboration environment for both parties, replacing the email back-and-forth with a real-time, visible mapping and action-item board accessible to all stakeholders at any time.
⚡
Risk of the Status Quo
▾

Continuing the engagement on the current direct DB-to-DB path without the source artifacts and without methodology alignment carries three compounding risks.

Timeline Risk

Mapping without the DDL and MISMO samples requires validating every field by hand against inconsistent documentation. The output takes significantly longer to produce and still requires a re-validation pass once the real artifacts arrive.

Quality Risk

Field mappings produced without source artifact validation carry low to medium confidence at best. In a regulated data migration, low-confidence rows that are treated as final specs create downstream errors that surface during UAT, parallel testing, or post-go-live. At that stage, the cost of a correction is substantially higher than catching it now.

Audit and Accountability Risk

Without a shared, neutral tracking environment, blocked items, ownership disputes, and scope gaps live in email chains that are difficult to audit after the fact. When something goes wrong in a migration, the audit trail matters. The current workflow does not produce one.

Catching and correcting the approach now, before significant additional work is invested, is the right call. The groundwork completed to date positions the engagement to move quickly once these three approvals are in place.
